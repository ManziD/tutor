Differentiating Pure and Applied Mathematics in Uganda's New UNEB A-Level Curriculum: My Approach as a Teacher

By Manzi Delick · Kampala, Uganda

I once had a student who was very strong in calculations but avoided word problems completely. They believed applied mathematics was "too hard" and preferred straightforward algebraic manipulation. Instead of forcing complex applications immediately, I began linking each word problem back to a simple pure concept they already understood. Gradually, their confidence grew. By the end of the term, applied questions had become their strongest area. What changed was not their ability — but their perspective.

This experience reflects a major shift in Uganda's evolving education landscape. Under the new UNEB Advanced Level (A-Level) curriculum for Principal Mathematics, the distinction between pure and applied mathematics is no longer rigid. While older systems treated them as separate domains, the updated curriculum integrates them more deeply — and importantly, word-based, scenario-driven questions are now the norm across both papers.

Even traditionally "pure" mathematics questions are increasingly presented in contextual or structured formats. Students are expected not only to compute and prove but also to interpret, analyze, and apply concepts within given situations. This makes it essential for learners to develop both abstract reasoning and contextual understanding simultaneously.

Pure vs. Applied Mathematics: How I Break It Down for My Students

Pure Mathematics is the foundation — it deals with abstract structures, logical reasoning, and theoretical depth. It answers why and how things work through proofs, derivations, and conceptual clarity. In the new curriculum, this dominates much of Paper 1, though often framed in structured or guided scenarios.

Core areas I emphasize include:

  • Algebra (indices, logarithms, equations and inequalities, partial fractions, polynomials)
  • Calculus (differentiation, integration, series)
  • Geometry and Trigonometry (coordinate geometry, vectors)
  • Numerical concepts

I teach students to focus on precision, logical flow, and proofs — skills that build confidence in handling abstract problems, even when presented in unfamiliar contexts.

Applied Mathematics takes those pure tools and applies them to real-world modeling, prediction, and problem-solving. It focuses on interpretation, context, and practical outcomes. Applied elements feature strongly in Paper 2 and across integrated topics.

Key areas include:

  • Mechanics (dynamics, forces, motion)
  • Statistics and Probability (descriptive statistics, scatter diagrams, correlations, permutations and combinations)
  • Data analysis and real-life applications (optimization, rates of change in physical or economic contexts)

How I help students differentiate them clearly:

  • Pure: Abstract, proof-heavy ("Prove that...", "Show that..."), but now often embedded in structured or guided scenarios
  • Applied: Contextual and interpretive ("Calculate and interpret...", "Model this scenario..."), involving units, data, and real-life implications
  • The Integration: In the new curriculum, both are intertwined. A student must read carefully, extract meaning, and then apply the correct mathematical tools — whether the question appears pure or applied at first glance

My Personal Strategies for Preparing Students

  • Strong foundational sequencing: I ensure students master pure concepts thoroughly before applying them immediately to mechanics, statistics, or real scenarios
  • Emphasis on interpretation: Since questions are now more word-based, I train students to break down scenarios, identify key variables, and translate words into mathematical expressions
  • Intensive past paper practice: Timed UNEB-style papers weekly, followed by detailed marking and error analysis
  • Problem-based and contextual teaching: Using Ugandan and East African examples to make concepts relatable
  • Targeted skill building: Proof drills for pure mathematics; modeling and interpretation exercises for applied sections
  • Spiral revision and integration: Topics revisited continuously from both pure and applied perspectives
  • Exam technique mastery: Time management, question interpretation, and clear presentation
  • Building resilience and discipline: Encouraging consistent daily practice and a growth mindset

Final Message

The new UNEB A-Level Principal Mathematics curriculum demands more than just computation — it requires interpretation, adaptability, and depth. The rise of scenario-based questions means students must become thinkers, not just calculators.

By strengthening pure foundations and embracing applied reasoning, learners can navigate this shift successfully and build skills that extend far beyond the examination room.
